Inspector General Announces Landmark
Corporate Integrity Agreement with Vencor, Inc.

June Gibbs Brown, Inspector General of the Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS) announced today that the Office of Inspector General (OIG) has entered into a Corporate Integrity Agreement (CIA) with Vencor, Inc., one of the nation's largest operators of nursing homes and hospital services. Under the agreement, which is unprecedented in its focus on ensuring excellence in the delivery of long-term care, Vencor will adopt a comprehensive internal quality improvement program at the corporate, regional and facility levels. The agreement with the OIG resolves in part the ongoing investigations by the OIG and the Department of Justice involving allegations of poor quality of care and billing abuses at Vencor.

The OIG imposes compliance obligations on health care providers when negotiating its settlements of Federal health care program investigations arising under a variety of false claims statutes. A provider consents to these obligations as part of the civil settlement and/or in exchange for the OIG's agreement not to exclude that health care provider from participation in Medicare, Medicaid and other Federal health care programs. This administrative sanction is based on the OIG's authority to exclude providers who have submitted false claims to these programs.

The Vencor CIA is for a term of five years and requires the company to implement a variety of compliance measures to ensure the integrity of Federal health care program claims submitted by the provider. Modeled after the recently released OIG "Compliance Program Guidance for Nursing Facilities," the agreement addresses the quality of care delivered to residents and patients, as well as promotes compliance with the Federal health care program requirements. In addition to creating a quality assurance infrastructure, Vencor will engage a team of independent monitors selected by the OIG to provide an ongoing assessment of these systems and quality of care measures. For example, the monitors will review and analyze Vencor's policies, procedures and operations, along with the individual facilities internal reports, staffing data and assessments of patient and resident care. The monitors will work collaboratively with Vencor and the OIG to identify and correct system deficiencies and to enhance existing quality improvement programs.

Additional measures in the CIA require Vencor to:

  • conduct training each year for all employees to assure compliance with federal health care requirements,
  • continue to operate an internal compliance hotline,
  • create a comprehensive internal quality improvement program which will include the review of quality-related data, direct quality improvement activities and implement and monitor corrective action plans,
  • enhance the current system of internal financial controls to promote compliance with federal health care program requirements on billing and related financial issues,
  • retain an independent review organization to evaluate the integrity and effectiveness of the Company's internal systems and report annually its findings to the OIG.

"We commend the new management team at Vencor for working with us to develop an internal quality improvement model that we hope will become the standard for the long-term care industry," said Inspector General June Gibbs Brown. "We have had serious concerns about the company's past performance and believe that significant changes needed to be made. Vencor's engagement of the quality monitor and implementation of the CIA demonstrate the company's commitment of resources to providing quality care to nursing home residents. This is an excellent example of how government and the private sector can effectively collaborate toward a common goal--improving the care provided to our senior citizens."

"Through this independent monitoring, Vencor is taking steps that would help us and the states to ensure that nursing-home residents and hospital patients receive quality care," HCFA Administrator Nancy-Ann DeParle said. "This agreement should serve as a model for preserving quality of care and the safety of beneficiaries for corporations in similar circumstances."

Vencor has filed for protection from its creditors under Chapter 11 of the Bankruptcy Code. The terms of this agreement will not be effective until there is a settlement of the government's ongoing investigations of the company and the agreement is approved by the U.S. Bankruptcy Court.
