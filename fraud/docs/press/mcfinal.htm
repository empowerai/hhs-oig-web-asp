Inspector General Issues Final Compliance Program Guidance
for Medicare+Choice Organizations

The Department of Health and Human Services' Office of Inspector General today issued final guidance to help Medicare+Choice managed care plans design effective voluntary compliance programs to prevent fraud, waste and abuse in government health programs.

"Prevention is an essential element of the government's anti-fraud and abuse campaign, and this voluntary guidance will help the managed care industry understand the government's expectations for a well-run, effective compliance program," Inspector General Brown said.

The guidance -- Compliance Program Guidance for Medicare+Choice Organizations Offering Coordinated Care Plans -- was first published in draft form in the June 24, 1999, Federal Register and had a 30-day comment period. The final guidance will be published soon as a Federal Register notice.

"We are committed to providing guidance to the health care industry to help providers comply with the law," Brown said. "Since February 1997, the Office of Inspector General has issued voluntary compliance guidance for seven sectors of the health care industry -- clinical laboratories, hospitals, home-health agencies, third-party medical billing companies, durable-medical equipment suppliers, hospices and now for Medicare+Choice Organizations."

Similar to other guidance already issued by the Office of Inspector General, the Medicare+Choice guidance is based on the following seven elements: 1) implementation of written policies, procedures and standards of conduct; 2) designation of a compliance officer; 3) development of training and education programs; 4) creation of a hotline or other measures for receiving complaints and procedures for protecting callers from retaliation; 5) performance of internal audits to monitor compliance; 6) enforcement of standards through well-publicized disciplinary directives; and 7) prompt corrective action to detected offenses.

While Medicare+Choice organizations are required by the Health Care Financing Administration (HCFA) to have a compliance plan that includes the seven key elements cited earlier, the regulation gives substantial discretion to the Medicare+Choice organizations about how to implement the requirement. The Office of Inspector General's guidance is voluntary and offers informal advice to Medicare+Choice organizations in implementing their plans.

"We applaud the Inspector General for developing this guidance to encourage plans to comply with federal requirements for marketing, enrolling and caring for the millions of Medicare beneficiaries who choose Medicare+Choice plans," HCFA Administrator Nancy-Ann DeParle said. "Plans can readily adapt the elements of this guidance to meet Medicare's requirement that they maintain an effective compliance program."

The guidance identifies specific compliance risk areas for Medicare+Choice organizations, including marketing practices; enrollment and disenrollment methods; underutilization and quality of care; data collection and submission processes; the anti-kickback statute and other inducements; and the provision of emergency services to enrollees.

More than 6 million of Medicare's 39 million beneficiaries -- about 16 percent -- are enrolled in Medicare+Choice coordinated care plans, which are paid a fixed monthly amount for each beneficiary's care. Unlike traditional fee-for-service Medicare, where the financial incentives can prompt the delivery of unneeded care, the financial incentives for Medicare+Choice organizations are just the opposite. To safeguard against inappropriate practices, the guidance recommends that Medicare+Choice organizations, for example:

  • Take special care to ensure that all marketing materials are complete and accurate and include information about the limits on beneficiaries' choice of providers and that beneficiaries' enrollment in a specific plan can be terminated if the plan or the government chooses not to renew its contract.
  • Refrain from marketing only to healthy beneficiaries -- known as "cherry-picking" -- while avoiding beneficiaries with a high risk for costly or prolonged treatment needs. Examples of inappropriate practices in this area include asking beneficiaries medical questions before enrollment or holding marketing sessions for beneficiaries at sites where healthy enrollees would be more likely to be present such as health and exercise clubs.
  • Make sure that beneficiaries -- especially those with extensive care needs -- are not encouraged to disenroll from the managed care plan.
  • Ensure that all necessary covered services are available and accessible to all beneficiaries. Plans should also prohibit interference with health professionals' advice to patients and make sure their physician payment arrangements don't provide incentives to limit medically necessary care to any particular enrollee.
  • Make sure information submitted to the government -- especially information associated with enrollment, encounters or payment -- is accurate, complete and truthful.
  • Have policies in place to ensure that any arrangements with hospitals to provide emergency services for beneficiaries don't interfere with requirements for hospitals to provide screening and stabilizing treatment required by federal law under the anti-dumping statute.
