Department of Health and Human Services

Office of Inspector General -- Fact Sheet

OPERATION RESTORE TRUST OBJECTIVES AND FIRST-YEAR ACCOMPLISHMENTS

Contact: HHS Press Office
(202) 690-6343
May 13, 1996

Overview: Operation Restore Trust, the special HHS initiative against fraud, waste and abuse, was launched by President Clinton in May 1995. It was designed to demonstrate new partnerships and new approaches in finding and stopping fraud in the Medicare and Medicaid programs.

As a demonstration project, Operation Restore Trust was targeted at three areas of high spending growth: home health agencies, nursing homes and durable medical equipment suppliers. The efforts were targeted at five states comprising more than a third of all Medicare and Medicaid beneficiaries: New York, Florida, Illinois, Texas and California.

Special objectives for Operation Restore Trust included:

o Identify and penalize those who willfully defraud the Medicare and Medicaid programs

o Identify systemic problems and special vulnerabilities to fraud and abuse in the Medicare and Medicaid programs

o Alter the public and health care sector to health care fraud schemes

o Demonstrate a new effort which provides especially for voluntary disclosure of evidence of fraud.

Milestones have been reached in each of the objective areas, and the Clinton Administration plans to expand its reach to every State in the nation.

Objective: Identify, investigate and penalize those entities who willfully defraud the Government.

Accomplishments After One Year:

o Over $24.5 million are making their way back into the Medicare Trust Fund as a result of court ordered criminal restitutions, fines, and recoveries.

o $14.1 million are making their way back into the Trust Fund as a result of civil judgements, settlements and civil monetary penalties.

o 272 cases are currently under investigation, most of which could never have been pursued without the stable source of funding project ORT has provided. 244 of these are new cases that were opened as a result of the Operation Restore Trust initiative.

- 64 of these cases are joint efforts with other law enforcement agencies.

o 35 criminal convictions and 18 civil judgements have been obtained since March 1, 1995, in cases folded into the ORT project and benefiting from the increased attention, focus, and support provided by ORT.

o 93 program exclusions of individuals and corporations have been taken.

Examples:

o Frank Aiello, a nursing home owner and operator in California, was found guilty and sentenced on 28 counts relating to a multi-million dollar Medicare fraud scheme. Aiello billed Medicare approximately $3.5 million for nonexistent medical supplies for his nursing home, and filed false cost reports. Aiello also gave gratuities to two intermediary employees to avoid adverse audit findings. In January 1996, Aiello was sentenced to 11 years, 3 months in jail, ordered to pay $3,248,008 in restitution, fined $300,000, and ordered to pay a special assessment of $1,400. Three other individuals were also convicted and sentenced in connection with the scheme.

o On March 5, 1996, formerly licensed psychiatrist Thomas Sargeant was sentenced in California to 18 months in prison and ordered to perform community service in lieu of restitution for defrauding Medicare. Sargeant billed Medicare more than $100,000 for fictitious psychotherapy services to nursing home patients.

o U.S. HomeCare, a home health agency in Florida, signed an agreement to pay $650,000 to the Government. It was alleged that HomeCare knowingly submitted or caused to be submitted claims for payment under Medicare for home health services in violation of the False Claims Act. HomeCare did this through a series of subcontracts with home and other health care services on behalf of HomeCare. Subcontractors would bill HomeCare and HomeCare would fraudulently bill Medicare. In the event of default by HomeCare on its obligations, the Government may at any time and at its option exclude HomeCare from participation in the Medicare program and all State health care programs.

Objective: Identify problems of noncompliance with existing statutes and regulations; and identify systematic problems relating to program effectiveness and efficiency, and vulnerabilities to fraud and abuse.

Accomplishments After One Year:

o $3.7 million returned to the Treasury as a result of services inappropriately billed to ineligible persons, or medically unnecessary services rendered.

o Identified substantial amount of Trust Fund money that could be saved through program changes preventing fraud, waste and abuse--action the Administration is now considering for possible legislative, regulatory, and administrative proposals.

o New methods are being tested to involve State and local officials at the grass roots level in identifying and reporting noncompliance.

Examples:

o An evaluation of variation in services claimed by home health agencies, and a targeted compliance review in Florida, identified potential savings of at least $5 billion over five years if new payment mechanisms and utilization controls were implemented for the Medicare home health benefit.

o Evaluations of payments made for enteral nutrients and durable medical equipment in nursing homes identified over $150 million in program savings possible from management actions now being taken to improve coverage decisions and implementation in those settings.

o An evaluation of payments for drugs used with nebulizers identified over $50 million in program savings possible from management actions now in development to improve Medicare's drug purchasing policies. These drugs are covered under the Medicare program only in conjunction with durable medical equipment such as nebulizers. Since such policies also would affect other drugs purchased by Medicare, the total savings would be considerably more than that associated with nebulizer drugs alone, demonstrating the very positive "spillover" effects of Operation Restore Trust.

o Audits of three home health agencies in Florida identified $3.7 million in potential disallowances of costs claimed by providers. In one home health agency, auditors randomly selected for review 100 claims submitted for Medicare reimbursement during the fiscal year ending December 31, 1993. These claims represent 2,068 home health services. The review showed that 40 claims, or 40 percent of our sample, contained 846 services that did not meet Medicare guidelines for payment.

o HCFA developed an idea for innovative use of survey and certification staff to identify fiscal as well as quality problems and tested the concept in Texas, where it created 7 to 1 return in money recovered for the trust fund. In New York, the same concept was applied in targeting five home health agencies for review. Of fifty home health agency patients visited, nine patients were not homebound and 20 patients were receiving unnecessary or overutilized services. Many of the patients of one home health agency were residents of domiciliary care facilities, with possible duplication of services. At least 1 durable medical equipment supplier was providing excessive and/or unneeded supplies to home health agency patients.

o Over 500 State and local ombudsmen have been trained to identify and report suspected fraud, waste and abuse, a new process already resulting in referrals to the OIG for investigation.

Objective: Alert the public and industry to health care fraud schemes.

Accomplishments After One Year:

o Two special fraud alerts and one consumer advisory have been issued to the public and industry.

o A new hotline (1-800-HHS-TIPS) has been established, fielding over 30,000 calls from its inception, on a wide range of issues--not just those associated specifically with Operation Restore Trust--demonstrating the "spillover" effect of Project ORT. The Hotline has been promoted in and out of the ORT states through press releases, flyers, public appearances of HHS officials, Congressional correspondence, and Special Fraud Alerts.

o A new Miami satellite office has been established which, in addition to its other activities, is heavily involved in outreach to the local community to alert the public and local businesses to health care fraud schemes. The office, with bilingual staff, conducts outreach and educational activities. Staff have conducted an education campaign in the area, with meetings and briefings at town centers, seniors centers and other sites where seniors congregate. Bilingual flyers with the ORT 800-number and information about identified fraud scams have been distributed to better inform beneficiaries, their families and various organizations about specific fraud and abuse problems.

Objective: Allow for providers to voluntarily disclose evidence of fraud that they have uncovered in their own organizations.

Accomplishments After One Year:

o A new Voluntary Disclosure Program has been established. Through this program, the Office of Inspector General (OIG) has established procedures by which health care providers in the home health and nursing home industries may come forward with full disclosure of potential fraud and abuse for which the companies may be liable. The voluntary disclosure program is also available to home health and nursing home providers whose activities affect Medicare beneficiaries in the 5 target states. The voluntary disclosure pilot will last for the duration of the project.

In the first year of the voluntary disclosure program, the OIG has received a significant number of inquiries from outside counsel about the program. In several cases, the entity considering participation in the program did not qualify because it was not in the industries or states that Operation Restore Trust is targeting. Nonetheless, the entity came forward and, using the ORT disclosure program as guidance, disclosed self-discovered evidence of program fraud.

The creation of a mechanism for cooperative self-disclosure has produced several positive results. Through dialogue with health care industry representatives, the OIG has gained new insight on what are the obstacles to identifying and reporting incidents of program abuse. These insights will be used to reevaluate the program and to strengthen it. Of equal importance, the introduction of the concept of self-disclosure of fraud and abuse has presented new opportunities for Government and industry collaboration in resolving problems.

OPERATION RESTORE TRUST BACKGROUND

Overview:

Operation Restore Trust is a two-year effort to combat health care fraud, waste and abuse in the five states with the highest Medicare expenditures. The project reached its mid-point and one-year anniversary on May 3, 1996.

In "Operation Restore Trust," HHS designated an interdisciplinary project team of federal and state government representatives to target Medicare abuse and misuse in California, Florida, New York, Texas and Illinois. These states account for 38.5 percent of Medicaid beneficiaries and 34 percent of Medicare beneficiaries.

The team is focusing on home health care, nursing home care, and medical equipment and supplies, three of the fastest growing areas in Medicare. Three agencies within HHS -- the Office of Inspector General, the Health Care Financing Administration, and the Administration on Aging -- are involved, as is the Department of Justice.

Health Care Programs Affected

The two health care programs covered by Operation Restore Trust are Medicare and Medicaid.

Medicare is a federally administered program of health benefits for eligible elderly and disabled persons. Some relevant statistics include:

Total Medicare enrollees for 1995 was 37.7 million persons. 12.7 million persons, or 34 percent of the total Medicare enrollees, reside in the five Operation Restore Trust States.

1995 Medicare outlays were an estimated $177.8 billion.

Medicaid is a state-administered program for eligible recipients, substantially funded by federal government under title XIX of the Social Security Act. Some relevant statistics include:

Total recipients for 1994 projected at 35.1 million persons nationally. Medicaid recipients in Operation

Restore Trust States are 13.5 million, or 38.5 percent of the total.

Medicaid outlays for 1994 totaled $108.3 billion.

Areas of Focus Under Operation Restore Trust

Long-term Care Providers -- In 1995, there were 17,000 long-term care facilities nationwide, including skilled nursing facilities reimbursed under Medicare and nursing facilities reimbursed under state-administered Medicaid programs.

In 1994, Medicaid spent $27 billion for services to beneficiaries residing in nursing facilities; $9.5 billion in Operation Restore Trust States.

In 1995, Medicare directly reimbursed skilled nursing facilities $7.3 billion for care provided under the Medicare skilled nursing facility benefit. $2.7 billion was paid in Operation Restore Trust States.

Home Health Agencies -- Agencies are reimbursed by Medicare for certain services provided to homebound beneficiaries.

In 1995, Medicare payments totalled about $14.5 billion; compared to $12.5 billion in 1994. $5.3 billion was paid in Operation Restore Trust States.

In 1994, Medicare made payments to 7,800 home health agencies.

Medical Equipment and Supplies -- Medicare reimburses suppliers for specified items that are medically necessary for the care and treatment of illnesses of beneficiaries enrolled in Part B. Payments are made by 5 regional carriers under contract with the Health Care Financing Administration. Covered items include: (1) durable medical equipment, e.g., wheelchairs, walkers, oxygen concentrators; (2) prosthetic devices, (e.g., artificial limbs, catheters), and orthotic devices (e.g., braces); (3) supplies used with durable medical equipment or prosthetics, e.g., oxygen, cleaning solutions; and (4) supplies such as wound care dressings.

Medicare allowed about $4.5 billion in payments for medical equipment and supplies in 1995. $1.6 billion was paid in Operation Restore Trust States.

Ongoing Activities in Operation Restore Trust

o Financial audits by OIG and HCFA contractors

o Criminal investigations and referrals by OIG to appropriate law enforcement officials

o Civil and administrative sanction and recovery actions by OIG and other appropriate law enforcement officials

o Surveys and inspections of long-term care facilities by HCFA and state officials

o Studies and recommendations by OIG and HCFA for program adjustments to prevent fraud and reduce waste and abuse

o Issuance of Special Fraud Alerts to notify the public and the health care community about schemes in the provision of home health services, nursing care and medical equipment and supplies

o Fraud and abuse hotline

o Voluntary Disclosure Pilot Program

o Training of individuals within the aging network on how to identify and report suspected fraud and abuse
