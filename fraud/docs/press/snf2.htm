No Serious Access Problems Found for Medicare Nursing Home Patients,
But Careful Monitoring Needed

    A new nursing home payment system has not diminished Medicare beneficiaries' access to needed skilled-nursing care, but the effects of the new system require careful monitoring, according to a report issued today by the Department of Health and Human Services' Office of Inspector General.

    "So far, no serious problems in placing Medicare patients in nursing homes are apparent," Inspector General June Gibbs Brown said. "However, nursing homes are changing their admission practices in response to the prospective-payment system. This early assessment shows we need to continue monitoring to make sure beneficiaries receive the care they need and that nursing homes are paid accurately for Medicare services."

    The Balanced Budget Act of 1997 required payment for Medicare skilled-nursing facilities to change from a cost-based system to a prospective-payment system, which provides a fixed daily payment that varies based on a beneficiary's condition and expected medical needs. The new payment system began July 1, 1998, with most skilled-nursing facilities moving to the prospective-payment system on Jan. 1, 1999.

    To assess the new payment system's impact on beneficiary access to skilled-nursing facility care, the Office of Inspector General surveyed 180 randomly selected hospital discharge planners and analyzed Medicare data. The findings are contained in a report titled Early Effects of the Prospective Payment System on Access to Skilled Nursing Facilities.

    Most discharge planners -- 66 percent -- reported that it is "not at all difficult" to place Medicare patients in nursing homes, with some pointing out that Medicare pays higher rates than Medicaid. Another 32 percent said it is "somewhat difficult" to place Medicare patients, while only 1 percent reported that it is "very difficult" to place Medicare patients in nursing homes. About a fifth of discharge planners said it has become more difficult to place Medicare patients in the past year because of the new payment system but indicated they were able to place these patients.

    An analysis of Medicare data generally confirmed the discharge planners' views. The proportion of all Medicare hospital patients discharged to skilled-nursing facilities did not differ between the first five months in 1998, which is before the prospective-payment system was put in place, and the same five months in 1999, which is after the move to the new payment system. The review also did not show any large changes in the types of Medicare patients who were discharged to skilled-nursing facilities, with the 10 most common diagnoses remaining the same in both years. The total number of Medicare-certified nursing home beds increased 7.4 percent between 1996 and 1998 from 672,329 beds to 722,278 beds, which is a key factor in nursing home access.

    While about half of the discharge planners believed inpatient lengths of stay have increased for Medicare patients in the past year, Medicare data show no substantial increases in lengths of stay for Medicare patients with the 10 most common diagnoses who are discharged to skilled-nursing facilities.

    About half of all discharge planners surveyed reported that nursing homes have changed their admissions practices because of the new payment system. They say nursing homes are requesting more detailed clinical information about patients, looking more closely at the cost of care, and conducting more in-person assessments of patients before making admission decisions.

    When asked which types of patients have become more difficult to place, 58 percent of the discharge planners reported that Medicare patients requiring extensive services such as intravenous feedings, intravenous medications, or ventilator/respirator care have become more difficult to place in nursing homes in the past year.

    But 69 percent of the discharge planners reported that Medicare patients who need special rehabilitation, which is reimbursed at the highest rate under the prospective-payment system, have become easier to place in nursing homes in the past year. Discharge planners said patients with diagnoses such as joint replacements and strokes and patients requiring rehabilitation therapy such as physical, occupational or speech therapy have generally become easier to place.

    Two other reports released today provide more information about physical and occupational therapy provided to Medicare patients in nursing homes. The reports are based on medical record reviews of a national random sample of 218 Medicare patients and the physical and occupational therapy they received in March 1998, just four months before the new payment system went into effect.

    One report, Physical and Occupational Therapy in Nursing Homes: Quality of Care and Medical Necessity for Medicare Patients, found that most Medicare patients received appropriate therapy for their initial medical conditions and benefitted from therapy. However, the review also found that almost 13 percent of therapy was billed improperly to Medicare because the therapy was not medically necessary or was provided by inappropriate staff. Another 4 percent of therapy was not properly documented in the patients' medical records.

    The other report, Physical and Occupational Therapy in Nursing Homes: Cost of Improper Billings to Medicare, estimated that for the 12-month period ending June 30, 1998, Medicare paid skilled-nursing facilities almost $1 billion for improperly billed physical and occupational therapy and $331 million for improperly documented physical and occupational therapy. Additionally, the findings show that skilled-nursing facilities charged Medicare significantly more for contract occupational therapists than the nursing facilities paid for these services. These mark ups added an estimated $342 million a year to Medicare's costs for occupational therapy.

    The unnecessary and improperly documented therapy, as well as the occupational therapy mark-ups, were not identified and eliminated before the move to the new prospective-payment system, resulting in inflated base-year costs for the new payment system. Therefore, should any consideration be given to modifying the prospective-payment system, the identified inappropriate therapy costs should be considered, according to the report.

    The findings also have quality of care implications because some beneficiaries received too much or inappropriate therapy. The reports recommend that the Health Care Financing Administration adequately fund Medicare contractors to perform medical reviews of therapy and work with national therapy and nursing home associations to ensure that beneficiaries receive high-quality, needed care.
