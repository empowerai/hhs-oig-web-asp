Inspector General Proposes Safe Harbor to Protect Payment of Medicare Part B and Medigap Premiums for Financially Needy End-Stage Renal Disease Beneficiaries

    The Department of Health and Human Services’ Office of Inspector General today proposed a "safe harbor" to the civil money penalty statute to allow independent dialysis facilities to pay Medicare Part B and Medigap premiums on behalf of financially needy end-stage renal disease (ESRD) beneficiaries.

    While the Office of Inspector General has issued numerous safe harbors to the federal anti-kickback statute over the years, this is the first proposed safe harbor to the Inspector General’s civil money penalty authorities, which are codified in the Social Security Act.

    The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) added a new civil money penalty to the Social Security Act that prohibits providers from offering Medicare and Medicaid patients any inducement to order or receive items or services from a particular provider, practitioner or supplier. Payment of beneficiaries’ insurance premiums by Medicare providers clearly implicates the prohibition on inducements to beneficiaries.

    Passed in 1998, the Omnibus Consolidated and Emergency Supplemental Appropriations Act for Fiscal Year 1999 authorized the Office of Inspector General to establish safe harbors for payment practices that would otherwise run afoul of the civil money penalty for inducements to beneficiaries.

    To qualify for the proposed safe harbor, the following standards would have to be met:

  • The dialysis facility must be independent (i.e., not hospital-based) and not owned in whole or in part by a hospital, physician or other provider or supplier paid on a fee-for-service basis;
  • The premium payment must not be advertised;
  • The dialysis facility must not routinely make payments for such policies; and
  • The dialysis facility must make a good faith determination that the individual is financially needy.

    "I encourage Medicare beneficiaries, ESRD providers and other interested parties to comment on the proposed rule, so we can develop the best possible final regulation," Inspector General June Gibbs Brown said.

    The proposed rule especially invites comment on the potential impact on the Medigap insurance market and the exclusion from the safe harbor of hospital-based dialysis facilities and independent dialysis facilities owned in whole or in part by a hospital, physician or other provider or supplier paid on a fee-for-service basis.

    The proposed safe harbor went on public display today at the Federal Register and is scheduled for publication as a proposed rule in the Tuesday, May 2, 2000, Federal Register. There will be a 60-day public comment period from the date of Federal Register publication.

    Failure to comply with a safe harbor provision does not mean an arrangement is illegal. Rather, a safe harbor sets forth specific conditions that, if fully met, will assure that the entities involved will not be prosecuted or sanctioned for the arrangement qualifying for the safe harbor. Arrangements that do not comply with a safe harbor must be analyzed on a case-by-case basis for compliance with the civil money penalty statute.

    The Office of Inspector General has issued three advisory opinions — Nos. 97-1, 97-2 and 98-17 — dealing with payment of Part B and Medigap premiums for ESRD beneficiaries. The advisory opinions are available on the Office of Inspector General’s Web site at http://www.hhs.gov/oig/advopn/index.htm.
