SUMMARY REPORT RELEASED ON FIRST
OIG-HEALTH CARE INDUSTRY COMPLIANCE ROUNDTABLE

April 13, 1999--Washington, DC--For the first time, the Office of Inspector General (OIG) of the Department of Health and Human Services and the health care industry through the industry-leading Health Care Compliance Association (HCCA) conducted a joint roundtable on health care compliance to gain new insights into the challenges of creating effective compliance programs. A summary report on this recent meeting became available today on the OIG's web site (www.hhs.gov/progorg/ oig/new.html) and the HCCA's web site (www.hcca-info.org).

The March 22 Washington, DC event was a direct result of the OIG wanting to engage in constructive discussions with the health care compliance industry about various practices and policies related to compliance programs, including how to deal with compliance recommendations advanced by the OIG.

"We believe that these roundtable discussions have given us all a greater understanding of how the government and the provider community can work together to protect the integrity of the health care system," commented Inspector General June Gibbs Brown, who opened the one-day event.

Health Care Compliance Association President Brent Saunders, Esq., added, "We're excited that OIG chose to co-sponsor this program with HCCA, which furthers the commitment of HCCA to promote effective compliance programs throughout the industry."

Over 125 compliance officers, health care compliance consultants and government representatives attended the event, which was devoted to discussion of those issues of highest relevance to compliance professionals in meeting their professional objectives. Discussion groups focused on compliance professionals' and OIG's experience in four major areas:

  • Developing a compliance program;
  • Evaluating compliance effectiveness;
  • Conducting internal investigations and self-disclosures; and
  • Implementing a corporate integrity agreement.

Highlights of some of the most pressing areas in the compliance profession today are as follows:


Developing a compliance program:

Discussions in this area focused on such subjects as the scope of a compliance program, how to set priorities with a compliance program, and coordination of compliance efforts among different departments within an organization. For instance, to identify and respond to compliance risk areas, providers indicated they mostly focus on what the government highlights, as identified through OIG compliance program guidances, special fraud alerts, OIG work plans and fraud settlements.

Evaluating Compliance Program Effectiveness:

The topics addressed in this area of discussion included monitoring the functions of compliance programs and verifying their effectiveness. For example, government participants cited a number of factors to be considered in evaluating the effectiveness of a provider's compliance efforts, including:

  • Management's commitment to compliance, as measured by funding and legitimate support provided to the function;
  • Evidence of open lines of communication and the appropriate use of information lines to address employee concerns and questions; and
  • A documented practice of refunding of overpayments and self-disclosing incidents of non-compliance with program requirements.

Internal Investigations and Self-Disclosures:

Among the issues discussed in this topic area were determining the parameters and priorities of an internal investigation, the role of attorneys in internal investigations and the procedures for self-disclosure under the OIG's Provider Self-Disclosure Protocol.

To prioritize these investigations, participants recommended that compliance officers should consider such things as:

  • Does a corporate integrity agreement with the OIG require that the compliance officer focus on certain issues?
  • Could the issue under investigation have a significant impact on the provider's Medicare cost report and interim payments?
  • Does an issue present credible evidence of ongoing misconduct that may violate criminal, civil or administrative law and should be immediately reported to a government authority?

Corporate Integrity Agreements:

Discussion on corporate integrity agreements (CIA) included how the OIG incorporated the elements of a CIA into an existing compliance program, oversight of providers subject to a CIA, and the mechanics of operating under a CIA.

The Health Care Compliance Association is the only national non-profit organization solely dedicated to improving the quality of health care compliance. Its mission is to provide a collaborative forum promoting integrity and ethical behavior through the development of comprehensive compliance programs throughout the entire health care industry.

The mission of the Office of Inspector General (OIG) is to protect the integrity of the Department of Health and Human Services' (HHS) programs as well as the health and welfare of beneficiaries served by those programs. This statutory mission is carried out through a nationwide network of audits, investigations, and inspections conducted by three OIG operating components: the Office of Audit Services, the Office of Investigations, and the Office of Evaluation and Inspections. The OIG also informs the Secretary of HHS of programs and management problems and recommends courses to correct them.
